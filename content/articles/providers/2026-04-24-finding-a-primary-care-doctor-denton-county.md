# Finding a Primary Care Doctor in Denton County (2026)

A primary care doctor is the single most useful relationship in the American healthcare system and one of the easiest to let slide. Adults who have a regular primary care provider are significantly more likely to catch preventable illness early, manage chronic conditions well, and avoid emergency-room visits that could have been handled in a 20-minute office appointment (source: Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, 2024 National Healthcare Quality Report, ahrq.gov). And yet roughly one in three adults in Texas does not have a regular primary care provider, usually because they have not needed one recently, moved into the county without transferring care, or struggled to find a practice accepting new patients. Here is how to set up a primary care relationship in Denton County in 2026.

## Family Medicine vs. Internal Medicine

The first decision is which type of doctor to look for. A family medicine physician is trained to see every member of the household, from infants to grandparents, and often the same doctor can care for a whole family under one roof. An internal medicine physician (sometimes called an internist) sees adults only, usually starting at age 18, and is especially strong with adults who have multiple chronic conditions such as diabetes, hypertension, or heart disease. A pediatrician is an internal-medicine analogue for children and is the right call for families who want a dedicated kid doctor (source: American Board of Medical Specialties, abms.org). For a new-to-the-county family deciding between the two, family medicine is almost always the simpler starting point. For a single adult, either is a fine choice.

## Using Your Insurance's Provider Directory

The fastest filter is insurance. Most health plans sold in Texas cover in-network primary care visits at low or no out-of-pocket cost once the deductible is met, and visits with out-of-network doctors often cost hundreds of dollars more. Your plan's member portal has a searchable directory of in-network primary care providers with filters for specialty, gender, language, and distance from your address.

The directory is frequently out of date; doctors leave practices, close their panels to new patients, or change their insurance contracts more often than the insurance company updates the list. A reliable approach is to pull five or six names from the directory, call each practice, and ask three questions: Are you accepting new patients with my insurance? What is the wait for a first appointment? Who are the available doctors in that window? Most practice front desks will answer in a minute or two. Two or three phone calls usually surface a practice that fits.

## The Major Hospital-Affiliated Networks

Most primary care practices in Denton County sit within one of three major networks: Texas Health Resources, which operates Texas Health Presbyterian Denton and affiliated clinics at [texashealth.org](https://www.texashealth.org/); Medical City Healthcare, which operates Medical City Denton and affiliated clinics at [medicalcityhealthcare.com](https://www.medicalcityhealthcare.com/); and independent or small-group practices, including several long-established Denton family physicians. Both large systems have primary care clinics spread across Denton, Lewisville, Flower Mound, Highland Village, and Frisco. The advantage of a hospital-affiliated practice is integrated records, easy referrals to specialists, and labs and imaging that flow directly into the same chart. The advantage of a smaller independent practice is often shorter wait times and a more personal relationship with the physician.

## Sliding-Scale and FQHC Options

For uninsured patients or those on Medicaid, Health Services of North Texas (HSNT) operates the largest sliding-scale primary care network in the county. Clinics are located in Denton, Lewisville, and surrounding communities, and HSNT accepts Medicaid, CHIP, Medicare, private insurance, and cash on a sliding scale based on household income. Uninsured patients below 100 percent of the federal poverty level often pay a nominal fee per visit (source: Health Resources and Services Administration, hrsa.gov/opa). HSNT can be reached at (940) 381-1501 or [healthntx.org](https://healthntx.org/).

## Wait Times and New-Patient Intake

Expect a wait of two to eight weeks for a first appointment with an established primary care physician in 2026. Practices accepting new patients quickly are often newer physicians who are still building a panel, and this can be a good thing: a younger doctor with more appointment availability is often more attentive and has fewer administrative pressures than a veteran with a full panel. If you need urgent care while you wait for a new-patient appointment, retail clinics such as CVS MinuteClinic and hospital-affiliated urgent cares can handle acute issues like ear infections, UTIs, and rashes.

Before the first appointment, ask the practice to send an intake packet and request records from your previous provider. Bringing an up-to-date medication list, a summary of past surgeries, family medical history, and any recent lab results makes the first visit substantially more useful. A good primary care relationship usually takes two or three visits to establish. The work of the first visit is getting on the books and getting the basics into the chart.
