# Finding a Therapist or Counselor in Denton County (2026)

The hardest part of starting therapy is usually not the therapy itself. It is making the first call. Roughly 22 percent of adults in Texas reported needing mental health care in the past year but not receiving it, and the most common reason given was not stigma or skepticism but simple logistics: cost, insurance confusion, and not knowing who to call (source: SAMHSA 2024 National Survey on Drug Use and Health, samhsa.gov). Denton County has a wide network of licensed therapists, and the process of finding the right one is more straightforward than it first appears. The goal of this guide is to make that first call easier.

## Understanding the Credentials

Therapists in Texas practice under several different license types, and each one is qualified to provide talk therapy for most common concerns. A Licensed Professional Counselor (LPC) holds a master's degree in counseling and is licensed by the Texas State Board of Examiners of Professional Counselors. A Licensed Clinical Social Worker (LCSW) holds a master's degree in social work and is often especially strong in family systems, grief, and navigating complex life circumstances. A Licensed Marriage and Family Therapist (LMFT) specializes in couples and family work, although most also see individuals. A Psychologist holds a doctorate (PhD or PsyD) and can provide therapy and also psychological testing for conditions like ADHD and learning differences. A Psychiatrist is a medical doctor who prescribes medication, sometimes in combination with therapy and sometimes in coordination with a separate therapist (source: Texas Behavioral Health Executive Council, bhec.texas.gov).

For most everyday concerns such as anxiety, depression, grief, relationship stress, and life transitions, any of the first three license types is a good starting point. If medication is likely to be part of the picture, plan to see a psychiatrist or a psychiatric nurse practitioner in addition to a therapist.

## Finding Someone Who Takes Your Insurance

The single biggest filter on cost is whether a therapist is in-network with your insurance. A therapist billing $175 per session might cost you $25 with insurance if they are in-network, or the full $175 if they are out-of-network. Most therapists in Denton County list their accepted insurance plans on their profiles at [psychologytoday.com/us/therapists/tx/denton](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/therapists/tx/denton) and at [goodtherapy.org](https://www.goodtherapy.org/). Both directories let you filter by insurance, specialty, gender, language, and approach.

Your insurance company's member portal also has a search tool for in-network behavioral health providers. The directory is often out of date, so calling two or three names and asking directly is usually more reliable. A good first-call script is simple: "I am looking for a therapist for [concern]. Are you currently accepting new patients, and do you take [my insurance]? What is your session fee, and what would my out-of-pocket cost be with my plan?" Most therapists are used to this call and will answer in a minute or two.

## Sliding-Scale and Community Options

For uninsured patients or those whose insurance will not cover the therapist they want, Denton County has several sliding-scale and community mental health options. The Wellspring Center at Texas Woman's University operates a training clinic where graduate students in counseling provide therapy under faculty supervision at reduced rates, typically $10 to $30 per session (source: TWU Wellspring Counseling Center, twu.edu). The University of North Texas Psychology Clinic offers similar training-clinic services and testing at reduced rates at [psychology.unt.edu/clinic](https://psychology.unt.edu/clinic).

MHMR of Tarrant County Denton office and the MHMR Helpline at 1-800-866-2465 connect residents with the county mental-health authority, which serves adults with serious mental illness and children with serious emotional disturbance on a sliding scale based on income. For broader information on crisis lines, support groups, and community mental-health resources across the county, see our [mental health resources guide](/articles/resources/mental-health-resources-denton-county-2026).

## What to Ask at the First Call or First Session

Therapists are used to being interviewed, and a fifteen-minute phone consultation is common and usually free. It is appropriate to ask about their training, their typical clients, their approach (cognitive-behavioral, EMDR, psychodynamic, faith-integrated, and so on), and whether they have experience with the specific concern you are bringing in. If a therapist feels like a poor fit after one or two sessions, it is fine and normal to try someone else. Fit matters more than credentials.

Confidentiality is worth asking about too. Therapists are required by law to break confidentiality only in specific situations such as imminent risk of harm to self or others, or suspected abuse of a child or vulnerable adult. Everything else stays in the room.

## Faith-Based and Christian Counseling

For families looking for a therapist who shares their faith or integrates faith into the work, Denton County has several Christian counseling practices, and many mainline therapists also welcome faith as part of the conversation when the client brings it in. The Village Church, Denton Bible Church, and Gateway Fellowship each maintain referral lists of Christian therapists they trust and can point members and non-members to those lists on request.
