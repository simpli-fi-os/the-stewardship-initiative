# Childcare and Early Education in Denton County (2026)

Finding childcare is one of the most stressful tasks a working parent faces. In Denton County, where families range from young couples near UNT to established households in Flower Mound and Highland Village, the options span small home-based daycares, faith-based programs, national chains, and locally owned learning centers. The right choice depends on your child's age, your schedule, your budget, and the kind of environment where your child will thrive.

## What Childcare Costs in Denton County

The average monthly cost for full-time daycare in Denton is approximately $774, which falls slightly below the Texas state average (source: Care.com, Denton TX childcare costs, 2026). Across Texas more broadly, childcare costs range from $500 to $1,900 per month depending on the child's age, the type of facility, and whether care is full-time or part-time (source: Illumine, "Cost of Child Care in Texas," 2026). Infant care is almost always more expensive than toddler or preschool care because of the lower staff-to-child ratios required by state licensing.

For families on tight budgets, the Texas Workforce Commission administers a childcare subsidy program through Workforce Solutions for the North Central Texas region. Eligibility is based on income, family size, and employment or education status. Wait lists can be long, so applying early matters. Contact Workforce Solutions of North Central Texas or visit the [TSI family resources directory](/directory/family-services) for local information.

## Types of Childcare Available

Understanding the differences between facility types helps narrow the search.

**Licensed childcare centers** are commercial facilities regulated by the Texas Health and Human Services Commission. They must meet specific staff-to-child ratios, safety standards, and training requirements. Centers vary widely in curriculum, philosophy, and cost, but the licensing baseline provides a minimum standard. You can look up any center's inspection history through the HHSC childcare search at hhs.texas.gov.

**Licensed home-based daycares** operate out of a caregiver's residence. Texas licenses two types: registered child care homes (up to 6 children) and listed family homes (up to 3 unrelated children). Home daycares often feel more intimate and may offer more flexible hours, but the environment and quality depend heavily on the individual provider.

**Faith-based programs** are common throughout Denton County. Many churches and religious organizations run Mother's Day Out programs, preschools, and full-time childcare. Some faith-based programs are exempt from certain state licensing requirements, so it is worth asking directly about their oversight, staff qualifications, and safety protocols.

**Pre-K programs** through public school districts are free for qualifying four-year-olds. Denton ISD, Lewisville ISD, and other county districts offer half-day and full-day pre-K for children who meet income, language, military, or foster care eligibility criteria. This is one of the most underused resources in the county. Contact your local district's enrollment office to check eligibility.

## What to Look for When Visiting

A tour tells you more than a website ever will. When you visit a childcare center, pay attention to a few things that matter more than the paint on the walls.

Watch how staff talk to children. Are they at eye level? Do they use names? Are they engaged or distracted? The ratio of adults to children should meet or exceed Texas HHSC minimums: 1:4 for infants, 1:11 for toddlers, and 1:15 for preschoolers. Ask how long the lead teachers have been at the facility. High turnover is a red flag; children need consistency.

Ask about their sick policy, their approach to discipline, and how they communicate with parents during the day. Many centers now use apps to send photos, daily reports, and meal logs. Ask how they handle emergencies and whether all staff are CPR-certified.

Check the outdoor play area. Is it fenced, shaded, and age-appropriate? Children in full-time care spend a significant part of their day at the facility, and outdoor time is essential for development.

## Affordability Resources

If cost is the primary barrier, several programs can help. Beyond the Texas Workforce Commission subsidy mentioned above, some employers offer Dependent Care Flexible Spending Accounts (DCFSA) that allow you to set aside up to $5,000 per year in pre-tax dollars for childcare expenses. The federal Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit also offsets costs for qualifying families.

United Way of Denton County and other local nonprofits occasionally offer childcare assistance grants.
