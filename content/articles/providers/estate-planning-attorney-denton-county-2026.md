# Estate Planning Attorneys in Denton County (2026)

Most people know they should have a will. Far fewer actually have one. A 2024 Caring.com survey found that only 32% of American adults have an estate plan in place (source: Caring.com, 2024 Wills and Estate Planning Study). In a fast-growing county like Denton, where young families are buying homes and building businesses alongside retirees managing pensions and property, the gap between "should" and "did" carries real consequences. An estate planning attorney helps close that gap by turning your intentions into legally enforceable documents.

## What Estate Planning Covers

Estate planning is broader than writing a will, though that is where most people start. A complete estate plan in Texas typically includes several documents working together.

A **last will and testament** directs how your property is distributed after death and, critically for parents of minor children, names a guardian. Texas law requires that a will be signed in the physical presence of two witnesses to be valid (source: Texas State Law Library, Wills and Directives guide). If you die without a will in Texas, the state's intestacy laws decide who gets what, and those defaults may not match your wishes at all.

A **revocable living trust** allows you to transfer assets into a trust during your lifetime, which can help your family avoid the probate process after your death. Probate in Texas is simpler than in some states, but it still takes time and costs money. Whether a trust makes sense depends on the size and complexity of your estate. For many Denton County families with a home, retirement accounts, and modest savings, a well-drafted will may be sufficient. An honest attorney will tell you which approach fits.

A **durable power of attorney** authorizes someone you trust to handle your financial affairs if you become incapacitated. Without one, your family may need to go to court to get legal authority to pay your bills, manage your accounts, or sell property on your behalf.

A **medical power of attorney and advance directive** (sometimes called a living will) specifies who makes healthcare decisions for you if you cannot, and what kind of medical treatment you do or do not want. Texas has specific statutory forms for these documents, and they should be part of every adult's plan regardless of age or health.

## How to Choose an Estate Planning Attorney

Not every attorney who can draft a will should be your first choice. Estate planning touches tax law, property law, family law, and sometimes business law. A general practitioner who writes the occasional will may miss planning opportunities that a specialist would catch.

The Texas Board of Legal Specialization certifies attorneys in estate planning and probate law. Board certification is not required to practice in this area, but it signals that the attorney has passed additional testing and peer review. Ask whether the attorney holds this certification or, at minimum, whether estate planning constitutes the majority of their practice (source: Texas Trust Law, "Choosing the Right Estate Planning Attorney in Texas").

Ask about their fee structure. Many estate planning attorneys charge flat fees for standard document packages: a will, powers of attorney, and advance directive might cost between $1,000 and $3,000 depending on complexity. Trusts are typically priced separately. Flat fees are preferable because they let you ask questions without watching the clock. Hourly billing, common in litigation, creates a disincentive to communicate with your own attorney.

Ask whether the firm handles document execution. A will that is drafted but never properly signed and witnessed is legally useless. A good firm walks you through the signing ceremony and ensures everything is notarized and stored properly.

## When to Start

The honest answer is now. Estate planning is not something reserved for the wealthy or the elderly. If you own property, have children, run a business, or have any financial accounts with beneficiary designations, you have an estate. The question is only whether you have a plan for it or whether you are leaving those decisions to the state of Texas.

Major life events should trigger a review: marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, buying a home, starting a business, a significant inheritance, or the death of a named executor or guardian. Even without a triggering event, reviewing your documents every three to five years ensures they still reflect your family structure and your wishes.
