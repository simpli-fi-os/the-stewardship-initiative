# Finding a Pediatrician in Denton County (2026)

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that newborns see a pediatrician within three to five days of leaving the hospital, then again at one month, two months, four months, six months, nine months, and twelve months in the first year alone (source: AAP Bright Futures, brightfutures.aap.org). For new parents that means the relationship with a pediatrician starts before the baby is born and lasts roughly two decades. It is worth a few hours of careful selection on the front end.

Most Denton County hospitals will ask expectant parents to name a pediatrician during the third trimester so that the chosen doctor can be notified when the baby is born and can perform the first newborn exam in the hospital. The right time to start looking is around 28 to 32 weeks of pregnancy, or whenever you start interviewing OBs and tour the labor and delivery unit.

## What Board Certification Actually Means

A pediatrician has completed four years of medical school and a three-year pediatric residency. Board certification through the American Board of Pediatrics is voluntary and indicates the doctor has passed a comprehensive exam and continues to meet ongoing education requirements (source: American Board of Pediatrics, abp.org). Board certified pediatricians can use the credential FAAP, which stands for Fellow of the American Academy of Pediatrics.

Verify any pediatrician's credentials at [certificationmatters.org](https://www.certificationmatters.org/), the public lookup tool maintained by the American Board of Medical Specialties. The Texas Medical Board licensure lookup at [tmb.state.tx.us](https://www.tmb.state.tx.us/) shows whether a physician's Texas license is active and whether there have been any disciplinary actions.

## Independent Practices Versus Hospital Systems

Denton County has both independent pediatric practices and pediatric groups affiliated with the larger DFW hospital systems. Each model has tradeoffs.

Independent practices like Pediatric Associates of Denton and Sloan Pediatrics are typically run by small groups of doctors who own the practice. The pace tends to be a little slower, the front-desk staff often knows your child's name, and decisions about scheduling and policies are made by the doctors who see the patients. The tradeoff is that referrals to specialists and after-hours coverage rely on partnerships with outside providers rather than an integrated system.

Pediatric groups affiliated with Texas Health Resources, Cook Children's Health Care System, or Children's Health (the Dallas-based pediatric hospital network) operate more like a single coordinated system. Specialists are in-network, electronic records flow between primary care and the hospital, and after-hours nurse advice lines are usually staffed by the same system. The tradeoff is that the patient experience can feel more institutional and continuity with a specific doctor is sometimes harder to maintain.

Neither model is automatically better. Families with a child who has a complex medical condition often value the integrated specialist access of a hospital-system practice. Families with healthy kids often value the personal feel of an independent practice. The right answer depends on the family.

## Insurance and Cost

The first practical filter is your insurance plan's network. Most pediatric care is heavily insurance-dependent and the difference between in-network and out-of-network can run thousands of dollars per year for routine well visits, vaccinations, and sick visits. Call the practice's billing office before the first appointment and confirm both that the practice takes your plan and that the specific pediatrician you want is credentialed under that plan. Both pieces have to be true.

For families on Medicaid or CHIP, Texas Health Steps covers all recommended well-child visits and vaccinations at no cost through age 20. The list of participating Denton County pediatricians is available at [yourtexasbenefits.com](https://www.yourtexasbenefits.com/) or by calling (877) 541-7905. For families without insurance, Health Services of North Texas provides pediatric primary care on a sliding scale based on income at locations including 4400 Mesa Dr in Denton and 720 W Main St in Lewisville. More on those clinics is in our [free and low-cost healthcare guide](/articles/resources/2026-04-20-free-low-cost-healthcare-denton-county).

## Questions Worth Asking Before You Commit

Before you settle on a pediatrician, schedule a free meet-and-greet visit if the practice offers them, or ask these questions during a phone intake.

Ask about the practice's vaccine policy. Some practices will not accept families who decline routine immunizations on the AAP schedule. If you have specific concerns or questions about timing, raise them now rather than at a four-month visit.

Ask how same-day sick visits work. A pediatric practice that cannot see a sick child the same day or next day is functionally relying on urgent care, which is fine for occasional visits but expensive over a year.

Ask which hospitals the doctors have admitting privileges at. If your child needs to be admitted, you want a pediatrician who can follow up at the hospital your insurance covers.

Ask how after-hours questions are handled. Some practices have a 24-hour nurse line. Others rely on a phone tree that routes to the on-call doctor only for emergencies.
