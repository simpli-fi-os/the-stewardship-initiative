# Finding an OB-GYN in Denton County (2026)

An OB-GYN (obstetrician-gynecologist) is the physician most adult women see for an annual exam, contraception, prenatal care, delivery, and the broader set of reproductive health questions that come up across a lifetime. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends that most women establish a relationship with an OB-GYN or other women's health provider in their late teens or early twenties and continue that relationship through menopause and after (source: American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, acog.org). For a woman new to Denton County or due for a fresh provider, here is how the local landscape looks in 2026.

## OB-GYN, Midwife, or Women's Health Nurse Practitioner

Three types of clinicians can serve as a primary women's health provider. An OB-GYN is a medical doctor with four years of residency training in obstetrics and gynecology and is the right call for most adults, anyone with a high-risk pregnancy, and anyone needing surgery. A certified nurse-midwife (CNM) is a registered nurse with graduate training in midwifery, can deliver babies in a hospital or birth center, and tends to focus on low-risk pregnancies and a less-intervention birth experience. A women's health nurse practitioner (WHNP) provides routine gynecologic care including annual exams, contraception, and screenings but does not typically deliver babies. Most large practices in the county have all three working together, and a patient often sees a different clinician at different visits within the same practice (source: American College of Nurse-Midwives, midwife.org).

## Hospital Systems and the Major Practices

Most OB-GYN practices in Denton County are affiliated with one of two hospital systems. Texas Health Presbyterian Denton on Hickory Street and Medical City Denton on Colorado Boulevard each operate full labor-and-delivery units, and most local OB-GYNs deliver at one or the other (source: Texas Health Resources, texashealth.org; Medical City Healthcare, medicalcityhealthcare.com). The choice of practice usually determines the hospital, so for a woman planning a pregnancy and with a strong preference for one hospital, picking the doctor is also picking the delivery floor. Both hospitals have NICU coverage; Medical City Denton has a higher-level NICU for complex cases.

In south Denton County, Texas Health Presbyterian Flower Mound and Medical City Lewisville both have OB units, and several large practices including OB-GYN Associates of North Texas and the Texas Health Physicians Group practices serve patients across both hospitals. Most insurance plans contract with both systems, but it is worth confirming network status before booking a first appointment.

## Sliding-Scale and Safety-Net Options

Health Services of North Texas (HSNT) operates a women's health program at its Denton, Lewisville, and Plano clinics that covers annual exams, contraception, STI screening, prenatal care, and follow-up postpartum visits on a sliding scale based on household income. HSNT accepts Medicaid, CHIP perinatal coverage, private insurance, and cash, and uninsured patients below 100 percent of the federal poverty level often pay a nominal fee per visit (source: Health Services of North Texas, healthntx.org). HSNT can be reached at (940) 381-1501. For pregnancy-specific Medicaid (CHIP perinatal and Medicaid for Pregnant Women), enrollment runs through Your Texas Benefits or 211.

## Choosing a Practice for Pregnancy

For a planned or new pregnancy, the practice choice is one of the more important decisions of the next nine months. A few questions worth asking on the first call:

The practice's call schedule. Most practices share on-call coverage among partners, so the doctor a patient has been seeing throughout the pregnancy may or may not be the one on call when labor starts. Asking how the rotation works and how often one's primary OB is on call gives a realistic picture.

The hospital. A woman who feels strongly about delivering at one specific hospital should confirm the practice delivers there and not at the other one nearby.

VBAC, doula, and birth-plan flexibility. Practices vary significantly in how they handle vaginal birth after cesarean, doula attendance, and birth plans for low-intervention deliveries. A practice that does a high volume of midwife-attended births is generally more flexible than a high-volume surgical practice, although both can deliver safely.

C-section rate. The CDC publishes hospital-level cesarean delivery rates, and most practices will share their own rate if asked. The national NTSV (low-risk first-time mothers) cesarean target is 23.6 percent (source: U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, Healthy People 2030, health.gov).

## Wait Times and First Visit

Expect a wait of three to ten weeks for a first appointment with an established OB-GYN in 2026. New patients often get on the books faster with a younger physician who is still building a panel or with a women's health nurse practitioner inside a larger group. Pregnant patients are generally fit in faster, often within two to three weeks of confirming the pregnancy. The first visit usually includes a medical history, a physical exam, and a discussion of contraception or pregnancy goals; bringing a list of current medications, family medical history, and prior surgical records makes the appointment more useful.
