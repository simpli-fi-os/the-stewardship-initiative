# Free and Low-Cost Legal Help in Denton County (2026)

Most people who need a lawyer the worst cannot afford one. A family facing eviction, a parent trying to protect a child in a custody dispute, a veteran fighting a benefits denial, an hourly worker whose wages have been stolen. These are the moments where legal help is not optional, and where the cost of a private attorney is often out of reach. In Texas, the civil legal system assumes you will pay your own way, and the result is that many low-income residents face serious legal problems without representation at all.

This guide covers the organizations, clinics, and referral services that provide free or reduced-fee legal help to Denton County residents in 2026. Most of what follows is income-based, which means you qualify if your household income falls below a certain percentage of the federal poverty level. Bring recent pay stubs, a photo ID, and any legal paperwork you have already received.

## Legal Aid of NorthWest Texas

Legal Aid of NorthWest Texas, known as LANWT, is the primary civil legal aid provider for Denton County. It is a nonprofit funded by the Legal Services Corporation and the Texas Access to Justice Foundation, and it covers 114 counties across North and West Texas including Denton, Tarrant, Dallas, and Collin. LANWT serves households at or below 125 percent of the federal poverty level, with some programs reaching up to 200 percent (source: Legal Aid of NorthWest Texas, lanwt.org).

LANWT handles civil matters only, which means they do not take criminal cases. The most common case types include family law such as divorce, custody, and protective orders for domestic violence survivors; housing issues including eviction defense, substandard conditions, and foreclosure; public benefits denials for SNAP, Medicaid, Social Security, and unemployment; consumer protection matters like debt collection harassment and wage theft; and disaster recovery assistance after storms, floods, or tornadoes.

Applications can be started by calling the statewide intake line at (888) 529-5277 or through the online application at [lanwt.org](https://www.lanwt.org/). The Denton branch office is at 320 S Loop 288 Suite 104, but most intake is handled by phone. Wait times for non-emergency cases can be several weeks. Anyone facing an eviction hearing, a protective order deadline, or a court date within fourteen days should mention that during intake so the case can be prioritized.

Survivors of domestic violence can also access legal help through the resources covered in our guide to [domestic violence support in Denton County](/resources/domestic-violence-denton-county-2026), which coordinates closely with LANWT on protective orders.

## Texas RioGrande Legal Aid Veterans Program

Texas RioGrande Legal Aid operates a statewide Veterans Legal Services program that serves veterans in every Texas county including Denton. Services cover VA benefits appeals, discharge upgrades, landlord-tenant disputes affecting housing stability, and family law matters connected to military service. The program is free to eligible veterans and their families. Intake runs through (888) 988-9996 or [trla.org/veterans](https://www.trla.org/veterans). More general veteran resources are listed in our [veteran services guide](/resources/veteran-services-denton-county-2026).

## Denton County Lawyer Referral Service

Not everyone qualifies for free legal aid. Households above the income cutoff but still struggling to afford a private attorney can use a lawyer referral service, which connects callers to a vetted local attorney for a reduced initial consultation, usually around $30 for thirty minutes.

The State Bar of Texas runs a statewide service at (800) 252-9690 and [texasbar.com](https://www.texasbar.com/). It matches callers to attorneys by practice area and location, with all referred attorneys holding current malpractice insurance and active Texas bar licensure. After the initial consultation the attorney may quote a standard fee, take the case on a reduced-fee basis, or refer the client back to legal aid if income makes that a better fit.

## Court Self-Help Resources

Denton County court clerks cannot give legal advice, but they can direct residents to self-help forms and instructions. The District Clerk's office at 1450 E McKinney St in Denton maintains a library of approved forms for common matters including uncontested divorce without children, small claims filings, and name changes.

The State Law Library in Austin maintains the most comprehensive set of free Texas legal forms and self-help guides online at [texaslawhelp.org](https://texaslawhelp.org/). The site is a project of the Texas Access to Justice Commission and includes plain-language explanations of family law, housing law, consumer law, and court procedures, along with step-by-step instructions for representing yourself in common matters. Every document on the site is reviewed by licensed Texas attorneys.

For matters that require in-person help filling out forms, the Denton County Law Library, located inside the courthouse annex at 1450 E McKinney, offers supervised self-help time on weekday afternoons. Staff cannot give advice but can point to the correct form, explain filing fees, and provide computer access for people without internet at home.

## Free Legal Clinics

Several volunteer attorneys in Denton County hold free legal clinics throughout the year. First Refuge Ministries and several area churches partner with LANWT to host pro bono intake events that cover family law, benefits, and consumer issues. Clinic schedules change, so residents should call First Refuge at (940) 382-0580 or check [lanwt.org](https://www.lanwt.org/) for the current calendar.

Texas A&M University School of Law in Fort Worth operates a civil clinic where supervised law students represent low-income clients in family law, immigration, and benefits cases at no cost. Intake is competitive and limited, and the clinic focuses on cases that provide teaching value, but accepted clients receive full representation from a supervised student team at no charge.

## What to Bring to Any Legal Appointment

Every legal aid intake benefits from a simple prep list. Bring a government-issued ID, the last two pay stubs for every adult in the household, any court papers or notices already received, a list of the names and contact information of the other parties involved, and a short written timeline of what happened. Ten minutes of preparation often doubles what a volunteer attorney can accomplish in a thirty-minute clinic slot.
