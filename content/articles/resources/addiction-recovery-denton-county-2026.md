# Addiction and Recovery Resources in Denton County (2026)

Most people who eventually find recovery in Denton County did not start with a treatment center. They started with a phone call, a meeting in a church basement, or a conversation with someone who had been there. The path is rarely clean, and it is rarely linear. What matters is knowing the door is unlocked and that real help is closer than people think.

This guide is for the person who is ready to make a change, the family member who is exhausted and scared, and the friend who does not know what to say. It covers the local agencies, the free meetings, the crisis lines, and the practical first steps.

## If This Is a Crisis Right Now

If you or someone you know is in immediate danger from an overdose, call 911. Texas has a Good Samaritan law that protects people who call for medical help during an overdose from being prosecuted for small amounts of drugs found at the scene. Calling saves lives. Hesitating loses them.

For a non-emergency mental health or substance use crisis, the Denton County MHMR Center crisis line is (800) 762-0157. The line is staffed 24 hours a day, every day. Trained crisis workers can help over the phone, dispatch a mobile crisis team, or arrange transport to an emergency assessment.

The federal SAMHSA helpline at 1-800-662-HELP (4357) is another 24/7 option. It is free, confidential, in English and Spanish, and connects callers to local treatment resources. SAMHSA does not provide treatment itself; it routes people to providers.

## The Hub: Denton County MHMR

The Denton County MHMR Center is the primary publicly funded behavioral health provider in the county and the natural starting point for most people seeking help with substance use. The main office is at 2519 Scripture Street in Denton, and the main number is (940) 381-5000 (source: Denton County MHMR Center). MHMR offers free assessments, outpatient counseling, medication-assisted treatment for opioid use disorder, peer recovery support, and case management.

Cost is a common worry. MHMR uses a sliding-scale fee structure based on income, and many services are no-cost for those who qualify. You do not need health insurance to walk in for an initial appointment. You do need a phone call to schedule. The intake process is straightforward but can feel slow during high-demand months, so call early and follow up if you do not hear back within a week.

For people whose primary issue is mental health rather than substance use, see our [mental health resources guide](/articles/resources/mental-health-resources-denton-county-2026).

## Free Peer Support: AA, NA, and Beyond

For many people, the most powerful tool in recovery is sitting in a room with others who understand. Denton County has a dense calendar of free peer support meetings.

Alcoholics Anonymous holds dozens of meetings each week across the county, in Denton, Lewisville, Flower Mound, Highland Village, Sanger, and the smaller towns. Meeting times, locations, and formats (open, closed, women-only, men-only, candlelight, beginner) are listed at aa-dallas.org and on local AA websites. There is no fee, no membership requirement, and no commitment beyond a desire to stop drinking.

Narcotics Anonymous serves people recovering from any substance, not only narcotics. The North Texas Region of NA maintains a current meeting list at lonestarna.org. Many NA meetings in Denton County are held in church fellowship halls and community centers, and most are open to newcomers without notice.

For families and loved ones, Al-Anon and Nar-Anon provide a parallel framework focused on the person who is loving someone in active addiction. Both organizations have active Denton County meetings. The wisdom in those rooms is hard-won and freely given.

## Detox and Inpatient Treatment

A medical detox is sometimes necessary before recovery work can begin, especially for alcohol, benzodiazepines, and opioids. Detox from these substances can be medically dangerous and should not be attempted alone at home. The Denton County MHMR crisis line at (800) 762-0157 can help arrange a safe detox, including transport to a partner facility if a bed is available.

Inpatient and residential treatment programs in the broader North Texas region range from short-term (30 days) to long-term (90 days or more). Insurance coverage varies widely. The SAMHSA helpline can help locate programs that accept Medicaid, Medicare, or sliding-scale payment. Be cautious of any program that pressures families into immediate decisions, asks for large upfront cash payments, or guarantees outcomes. Reputable providers do not work that way.

## Medication-Assisted Treatment

Medication-assisted treatment, or MAT, combines FDA-approved medications such as buprenorphine (Suboxone), methadone, or naltrexone with counseling. For opioid use disorder, MAT is the standard of care recommended by the National Institute on Drug Abuse and is associated with significantly higher long-term recovery rates than abstinence-based treatment alone. Denton County MHMR offers MAT through its substance use program, and several private providers in the county also prescribe.

There is no shame in using medication to support recovery. A person on MAT is in recovery, full stop.

## When Recovery Comes With Other Hard Things

Addiction rarely shows up alone. Many people in recovery are also navigating job loss, housing instability, legal issues, or strained family relationships.

## A Note on Stigma

People in recovery are not their addiction. The single most useful thing a friend or family member can do is treat the person like a person, ask how they are doing, and listen without trying to fix it. Recovery often takes more than one attempt. A relapse is not a failure of character; it is information about what kind of support is needed next. Stay in the relationship if you can. The data on long-term outcomes is clear: connection beats isolation, every time.
