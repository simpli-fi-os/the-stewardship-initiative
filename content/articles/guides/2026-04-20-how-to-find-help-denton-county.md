# How to Find Help in Denton County When You Don't Know Where to Start

Most people who end up needing community assistance did not plan to. A medical bill, a lost job, a death in the family, a broken transmission, an eviction notice: one hard thing happens, and then the next hard thing happens before the first one is resolved. By the time a family realizes they need help, they are often too tired and too embarrassed to know where to call. This guide is for that moment.

Denton County has a strong network of nonprofits, faith-based organizations, and public agencies. The system is real, and it works. The hard part is not that the help does not exist. The hard part is that it is scattered across dozens of phone numbers, websites, and church basements. This guide walks through the fastest way to get oriented, what to say when you call, and what to bring with you.

## Start with 2-1-1

If you take one thing from this guide, take this. Dial 2-1-1 from any phone in Texas, or visit [211texas.org](https://www.211texas.org/). The line is free, confidential, and available 24 hours a day, seven days a week. It is operated by the Texas Health and Human Services Commission, and the database behind it is the largest and most current community resource directory in the state.

When a caller tells the 2-1-1 operator their zip code and what they need, the operator matches them to the closest agency with current funding. That last part matters. Most community programs operate on monthly funding cycles, and the same pantry or rent-assistance office can be out of money one week and fully funded the next. A human operator working from a live database will catch that detail in a way that a general web search will not.

You can also request a callback if the line is busy, and the operators are trained to handle crisis calls including domestic violence, suicide risk, and child safety concerns.

## Serve Denton: One Door to Many Nonprofits

Serve Denton is a coalition of Denton County nonprofits that share a single building at 306 N Loop 288, Denton. The concept is called a one-door model. A family can walk in with a broad need, and the intake team will route them to one or more of the 30-plus agencies housed on site. Member organizations include food pantries, housing help, adult education, addiction recovery, foster care support, immigrant services, job training, and family counseling.

For a family that does not know exactly what category of help they need, Serve Denton is the best in-person first stop. Walking in with a utility bill, an eviction notice, and an empty pantry, a family can often leave with appointments, vouchers, and a plan the same day.

More information is at [servedenton.org](https://servedenton.org/).

## The Right First Call By Situation

Sometimes you know exactly what you need, and the question is which number to dial first. Here is a starting map for the most common situations in Denton County.

**If you are facing eviction or behind on rent**, the first call is the United Way of Denton County rental assistance line, followed by Christian Community Action for residents in the southern part of the county and the Salvation Army for the northern part. [Affordable housing resources in Denton County](/resources/affordable-housing-denton-county-2026) covers the full list of programs.

**If you need groceries this week**, the fastest path is the Tarrant Area Food Bank community resource line at (866) 430-6143, which serves the Denton area, or Christian Community Action's food pantry for southern Denton County. [Free food assistance in Denton County](/resources/free-food-assistance-denton-county-2026) covers pantries by geography.

**If you have a shutoff notice**, call the utility company first and ask about a payment plan. Then call 2-1-1 or Community Services Inc. to apply for the Comprehensive Energy Assistance Program. The full playbook is in [utility and energy bill assistance in Denton County](/resources/utility-bill-assistance-denton-county-2026).

**If you or a family member are in a mental health crisis**, the Denton County MHMR Center crisis line at (800) 762-0157 is staffed 24 hours a day. Walk-in crisis screenings are available at 2519 Scripture St, Denton. For broader mental health resources and non-crisis care, see [mental health resources in Denton County](/resources/mental-health-resources-denton-county-2026).

**If you need medical care without insurance**, Health Services of North Texas is the primary sliding-scale clinic serving the county, with locations in Denton, Lewisville, and Plano. [Free and low-cost healthcare in Denton County](/resources/free-low-cost-healthcare-denton-county-2026) covers the full picture.

**If you are in an unsafe home**, Denton County Friends of the Family operates a 24-hour hotline at (940) 382-7273 with emergency shelter and legal advocacy. The full resource list is at [domestic violence support in Denton County](/resources/domestic-violence-support-denton-county-2026).

**If you are a veteran or military family**, both the VA and local veteran service organizations have programs covered in [veteran services in Denton County](/resources/veteran-services-denton-county-2026).

## What to Bring When You Ask for Help

Almost every agency in Denton County that provides financial or material assistance will ask for similar paperwork. Gathering the basics ahead of time will turn a two-visit process into a one-visit process at most agencies.

Bring a photo ID for every adult in the household, Social Security cards or ITIN letters for every household member when available, proof of address such as a recent utility bill or lease, the most recent 30 days of income documentation including pay stubs, benefits letters, or child support records, and the bill or notice you are seeking help with. If you are asking for medical or behavioral health services, bring a list of current medications and any recent medical records you have access to.

If you are missing some of these documents, ask anyway. Many agencies will provide partial assistance while you gather what you need, and some can help you obtain replacement documents at no cost.

## A Note on Dignity and Timing

Two things tend to slow people down from asking for help. The first is the feeling that the family next in line probably needs it more. The second is the assumption that the application process will be so long and humiliating that it is not worth it.

Neither is true in practice. The agencies staffed across Denton County are there because their boards believe that preventing a crisis is cheaper, more effective, and more humane than responding to one. Most intake conversations are 30 minutes or less. Most staff members answering the phone have been trained specifically to be warm with people who are in hard moments.

The worst time to ask for help is one month after the moment you needed it. Shutoff notices, eviction filings, and health problems all get more expensive as they age. If you are reading this and wondering whether to call, call. The number is 2-1-1. The people who answer have been waiting for your call and will know what to do.
